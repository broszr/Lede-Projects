Funding A Governor

By Roman Broszkowski

Running for office is expensive, running for state-wide office doubly so.

Where does all that money come from? Some contributions come from Americans abroad or from US territories, but the vast majority come from contributors dispersed across these 50 united states.

But how dispersed are those contributors really? And what, if anything, can their patterns tell us about our politics?

50 states means 50 governors — 50 governors who've run at least 50 campaigns. That's a lot of contribution data! By plotting the states from which these contributors come, we can see that donors in most states gave to the governors of most other states. Most — not all. In fact, only contributors from Illinois and the District of Columbia gave to every single governor analyzed here.

While Illinois and DC are outliers, they're outliers not by much. On average, at least one donor from any given state (and DC) appeared in 84% of gubernatorial races.

Now there are a few caveats and disclaimers that go with that (check the README.md) — but still, 84% is quite high!

But while every state may have contributing donors, how much those donors give isn't the same. In some states, contributions average around $100, while in others, it's several thousand. The total amounts also vary wildly between states.

Across the 50 races for governor, the winning candidates brought in a total of $1.35 billion, yet roughly a quarter of that came from contributors in just Illinois and Texas. Another quarter came from donors in Florida, DC, Georgia, and New York. That means that contributors from just 12% of states (and DC) are responsible for half of all the money raised.

The fact that DC keeps popping up in this data, despite having a smaller population than 48/50 states, is surprising. Even more surprising is that DC leads the nation in the size of the average contribution - $7,288.13.

That DC money has ended up in all sorts of campaigns across the country.

So did contributions from Illinois, Texas, and New York. and California, and ..... well, you get the idea.

On the surface, the states where governors accepted a lot of out-of-state contributions don't have a lot in common. Some — like Arizona and Pennsylvania — are swing states where governors were in competitive elections, but others — like South Dakota — were very much not that.

The partisan lean of a state — how Democratic or Republican a state's population is compared to the country as a whole — also doesn't seem to explain how much out-of-state money a governor might bring in. Montana (R+10) and Washington State (D+10) saw significantly less outside contributions compared to Mississippi (R+11) or California (D+13). Is it all just random?

Battleground states

Democrats won these Republican-leaning battleground governorships. In these races, a significant number of contributors came from outside of the state and particularly, from Democratic-leaning states.

Contributors from Republican-leaning states like Texas, Florida, and Ohio made up a disproportionately large portion of the total donors in these Western, Midwestern, Upper Southern, and Appalachian states.

No! Obviously Not!

By creating a heat map of how donors in each state contributed to candidates in every other state and sorting by partisan lean, we can finally see the patterns.

Foremost, we see that donors from Democratic-leaning states like California and New York contributed heavily to successful Democratic candidates in key swing states, while donors from Republican-leaning states like Texas were influential in states where the Democratic Party has been on a significant decline in the last 20 years.

In other words, we reached a very obvious — but no less important — conclusion: political strategy guides political giving.
